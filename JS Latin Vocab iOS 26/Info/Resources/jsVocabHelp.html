These are flashcards representing the vocabulary to be learned in the course of studying the excellent first-year Latin textbook Reading Latin, by Peter V. Jones and Keith C. Sidwell, Cambridge University Press (thus “Jones and Sidwell”, or “J&S”).

The cards are sorted in the order in which vocabulary is presented in the textbook. Swipe, or tap at the right or left edge of a card, to navigate to the next or previous card. You can also tap a lesson number or section letter to navigate to the first card of the next lesson or section, and you can tap a part of speech category to navigate to the next part of speech.

Use the Key button to toggle display of the English translation. This is good for self-testing on vocabulary memorization.

Use the Speech button to toggle display of extra linguistic information accompanying a Latin term, such as principal parts of a verb or genitive (and possibly gender) of a noun. This is good for self-testing on this extra information. If you see an ellipsis (…) after a Latin term, the extra information is not showing.

Use the Folder button to select a section to jump to directly.

Use the Search button to view the entire vocabulary alphabetically.

Use the Bulb button to drill yourself on a subset of the cards, in random order.

A word about how the terms and definitions are displayed. On the whole I have followed Jones and Sidwell literally, including their punctuation and orthography, both for English and for Latin, where v’s are rendered as u’s (except majuscules) and are sorted with them. I have also followed their superb habit of displaying hidden quantity; in a few cases I have supplied macrons where J&S have accidentally omitted them. Please contact me if you find errors, and I’ll correct them.

In the matter of extra information to be learned with Latin terms, I have been less faithful. I am in favor of a minimalist approach, where the student is to memorize carefully just enough for formal mastery. For example, a student who has learned that the genitive of exercitus is exercitūs does not need to be told in addition that this noun is of the 4th declension (as this is obvious) nor that it is masculine (as all 4th-declension nouns are masculine, with rare exceptions such as manus, for which of course I do provide the gender). Thus I never display a declension number, and the only nouns for which I regularly show gender are non-personal nouns of the third declension — and even there I omit annotating obvious neuters of the tempus temporis pattern (and, late in the book, other obvious neuters as well). For verbs I do show conjugation number, because this is J&S’s way of stating the infinitive. Hyphens (to separate stem from ending) are omitted as distracting and obvious.
